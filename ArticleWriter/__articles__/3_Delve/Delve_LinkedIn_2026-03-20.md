Delve accused of faking SOC 2 and ISO 27001 reports for hundreds of clients. Here's what we know so far.

Yesterday, a long and detailed investigation landed on Substack accusing Delve — the YC-backed, $32M-funded compliance platform — of generating pre-written SOC 2, ISO 27001, HIPAA and GDPR reports at scale.

According to the investigation by DeepDelver — written by an affected Delve client who collaborated with others — a leaked Google Spreadsheet linked to approximately 575 confidential audit reports. The analysis claims that 493 out of 494 SOC 2 reports contained identical boilerplate text, with only company names, logos, and signatures differing. The investigation further alleges that auditor conclusions were pre-written before clients provided their company descriptions, which, if true, would constitute a violation of AICPA independence rules.

Delve disputes this. Back in December, CEO Karun Kaushik emailed affected clients calling the original leak notification an "AI-generated email" with "falsified claims." He attributed the exposure to human error on a single document and assured clients their reports remain valid. In January, Delve published a blog post titled "Inside Delve's Trusted Compliance Process," stating that reports are "triple verified" and "no two audit reports are the same."

Worth noting: this didn't come out of nowhere. The GRC Uncensored podcast had called out Delve's practices back in November 2025. The Daily Compliance newsletter reported on unnamed platforms selling template-based audit reports in January. And independent customer reviews have flagged that many of Delve's advertised integrations require manual screenshots rather than actual API connections.

To be clear — these are allegations from an involved party, and Delve has pushed back. No regulator has weighed in publicly. The full picture may look different once both sides have had their say.

That said, if you're using any compliance automation vendor — not just Delve — this is a good moment to ask some uncomfortable questions:

→ Request and review the actual audit report — not just a trust page or badge
→ Verify the independence and credentials of the auditing firm that signed the report
→ Ask your compliance vendor how evidence is collected, tested, and validated
→ Understand whether auditor conclusions are formed independently or generated by the platform
→ If your compliance certifications underpin contractual obligations or regulatory requirements (HIPAA, GDPR), confirm that the underlying process would withstand regulatory scrutiny

The compliance automation space promises speed. Sometimes that speed is real innovation. Sometimes it's cutting corners dressed up as efficiency. The hard part is telling the difference — and that's on buyers, not just the vendors.
